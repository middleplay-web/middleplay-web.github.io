Introduction

A short, engaging intro to Gorongosa’s environment and why it matters

Gorongosa is one of Mozambique’s most extraordinary natural places, filled with floodplains, forests, river, grassland and Mount Gorongosa’s rich rainforest habitats. It is home to thousands of plant and animal species and is known around the world as a powerful example of ecosystem recovery. Gorongosa matters not only because of its beauty and biodiversity, but because protecting it also supports science, conservation, and local communities. Every responsible visitor helps preserve this special place for future generations.

Visitor Guide

Rules and guidelines for visitors

What to do and not do!

What to do:

1. Follow your guide’s instructions

Always listen to park rangers and guides. They are trained to keep you safe and minimize disturbance to wildlife.

2. Keep a respectful distance from wildlife

Observe animals quietly and from afar. Use binoculars or zoom lenses instead of moving closer.

3. Stay on designated roads and trails

Driving or walking off-route damages fragile habitats and can disturb animals.

4. Take only photos, leave only footprints

Capture memories, but leave plants, rocks, and natural objects where you find them.

5. Dispose of waste responsibly

Carry out everything you bring in. Use designated bins where available.

6. Support local communities

Purchase locally made crafts, hire local guides, and respect village customs and traditions.

7. Travel quietly and patiently

Lower voices, silence phones, and enjoy the natural sounds of the park.

8. Respect park timings and rules

Enter and exit at approved times to protect both visitors and wildlife.

What not to do:

1. Do not feed wildlife

Feeding animals changes their natural behaviour and can harm their health.

2. Do not litter

Even biodegradable waste disrupts ecosystems and attracts animals to roads.

3. Do not leave your vehicle unless permitted

Predators and large animals can be dangerous, even if they appear calm.

4. Do not make loud noises

Avoid shouting, playing music, or revving engines. Noise stresses wildlife.

5. Do not remove plants, animals, or cultural items

Everything in the park is protected.

6. Do not smoke outside designated areas

Fires spread quickly in dry landscapes and threaten wildlife and communities.

7. Do not use drones without permission

Drones disturb animals and require special authorisation.

8. Do not approach local villages without guidance

Respect privacy and follow cultural etiquette.

Community

Respect for local communities

How visitors can be culturally sensitive

Conservation:

By protecting Gongorosa's beautiful animals and landscapes, we ensure future generations have a chance to experience this special place.

Respect for culture:

By providing educational programs, health care, and agricultural support to families–we improve the well-being of local communities.

Sustainable Tourism:

By developing sustainable tourism, we create employment for local people and generate sustainable revenue for the park. Every guest that visits gorongosa plays a vital role in this amazing conservation effort.

Interact thoughtfully

  • Greet people politely
  • Ask permission before taking photos of individuals or homes.
  • Be mindful that many villages are not tourist attractions; they’re everyday living spaces.

Be respectful with photography

  • Avoid “poverty tourism” — don’t photograph people in vulnerable situations.
  • Always ask first, especially with children.
  • Respect a “no” without pushing.

Respect land and resources

  • Don’t wander into villages or farmland without invitation.
  • Avoid wasting water or food—resources can be limited in surrounding areas.
  • Stick to designated park areas unless guided.

Rethink giving

  • Don’t hand out money, sweets, or gifts directly to children.
  • This can create dependency or disrupt community dynamics.
  • If you want to help, support structured programs run through the park or trusted organizations.

Learn before you go

  • Understand a bit about Mozambique’s history, including the Mozambican Civil War, which shaped the region.
  • Be aware of cultural norms (modest dress, respectful behavior in rural areas).

Bonus

Real-life examples of responsible vs. irresponsible tourism

1. Maya Bay, Thailand

Irresponsible tourism once involved large numbers of boats damaging coral reefs, tourists stepping on marine life, and pollution from fuel and trash. Because of this authorities introduced responsible tourism measures such as temporarily closing the bay, limiting daily visitors and preventing boats from landing directly on the beach.

2. Yellowstone National Park, USA

Responsible tourism means staying on boardwalks near geothermal areas and keeping a safe distance from wildlife. Irresponsible tourism occurs when visitors approach animals for photos, feed wildlife or walk off designated paths which can harm both people and the environment.

3. Barcelona, Spain

In Barcelona tourism also impacts the local community. Responsible tourism includes staying in licensed accommodation, supporting local businesses and respecting residents. Irresponsible tourism includes noisy behaviour, overcrowding, and short-term rentals reducing housing for locals, which has led to complaints and stricter tourism rules.
